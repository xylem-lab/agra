Overview

This report summarizes yield, acreage, and total production estimates for maize, rice, and beans across focus countries. The data is updated monthly in the RFBS Dashboard via a standard data file. Our model reports on the main growing season for countries with available historical yield data for model training.

Data Limitations and Model Caveats

The data used in this report has limitations due to gaps in historical records and variations in data collection methods:

  • Historical data accuracy and consistency vary by country
  • Our model reflects these data gaps, as it is trained on available information
  • We view these limitations as opportunities to improve data sharing and scaling methods with our country partners

Current Focus: Data presented this month focuses on maize, rice and bean systems in Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania, Malawi, Rwanda, Zambia, which are currently in season.

Executive Summary

Current projections for November 2025 maize, beans and rice production in East Africa show varying forecasts between USDA, FAO and UMD models, as summarized in Table and Figure below. For maize, USDA and FAO estimates for Tanzania are notably higher, with production around 7.0 to 8.0 million MT, while UMD projects a lower total between 2.11 and 3.49 million MT, reflecting its focus on a more limited geographic scope. Malawi and Zambia show closer estimates across sources, though UMD presents ranges indicating some uncertainty (e.g., Malawi maize from 2.83 to 4.05 million MT). Kenya and Uganda’s maize forecasts reveal some divergence, with UMD estimates generally falling between USDA and FAO values but with wider ranges (e.g., Uganda maize from 3.28 to 4.64 million MT by UMD vs. 2.8 to 5.0 million MT by USDA and FAO). Rice production in Tanzania and Kenya shows lower UMD estimates (e.g., Tanzania rice between 0.42 and 0.78 million MT) compared to USDA/FAO, likely due to model sensitivity to highland versus coastal zones. Beans are reported only by UMD, indicating moderate national outputs with relatively stable ranges, such as Uganda (1.01 to 1.49 million MT) and Rwanda (0.43 to 0.87 million MT). Overall, the differences highlight the impact of geographic coverage and methodological approaches, with UMD reflecting subnational variations and seasonal transitions, while USDA and FAO provide broader aggregated totals. The regional picture suggests generally stable to slightly varied production forecasts, emphasizing the importance of region-specific analysis for accurate agricultural planning.

Regional Overview/Conditions

Country Summary

  • Uganda: Beans exhibit stable conditions across all four regions with predicted yield anomalies ranging narrowly from -0.03 in Eastern to +0.05 in Western Uganda, indicating mostly neutral to slightly positive outcomes. Maize conditions present a mixed picture: Northern and Central regions show positive yield anomalies of +0.21 and +0.30 respectively, signaling improving or favorable trends, whereas Eastern and Western regions have notable negative anomalies (-0.22 and -0.30), highlighting areas of concern with declining trends especially in the Western region. Rice exhibits variable conditions with positive anomalies in Eastern (+0.31) and Central (+0.07) regions suggesting improving trends, but Western shows a significant negative anomaly (-0.27) reflecting declining conditions. The variability in maize and rice yield anomalies, particularly the pronounced declines in Western areas, flags a need for monitoring and potential intervention.
  • Kenya: Bean crops show a stable trend across all reported regions, with predicted yield anomalies mostly slightly negative to neutral, ranging from -0.06 to +0.10, indicating generally stable but slightly below average yield expectations in counties like Uasin Gishu and Nyamira. Maize displays mixed conditions: counties in arid and semi-arid zones like Wajir, Marsabit, and Kitui are experiencing significant negative yield anomalies from -0.61 to -0.54, reflecting declining trends and production stress, whereas maize in Rift Valley and western regions such as Uasin Gishu and Kakamega shows strong positive anomalies up to +0.65 with improving trends. Rice yields are stable to favorable across most regions with predicted yield anomalies between -0.28 and +0.36, notably stable in counties like Kakamega and Embu, with slight improving trends in Tharaka-Nithi and Embu. Overall, maize shows the widest range of conditions with some alarming declines in arid zones, while beans remain stable and rice mostly favorable.
  • Tanzania: Bean crop conditions across the unimodal Msimu season are generally stable with predicted yield anomalies near zero or slightly negative in regions such as Kigoma (-0.02), Songwe (-0.02), and Tabora (-0.01), while some regions like Rukwa and Ruvuma show slight positive anomalies up to 0.02. Maize conditions show mixed yield anomalies; some northern and unimodal regions like Rukwa (0.26) and Ruvuma (0.23) exhibit improving trends and positive anomalies, but large areas including Mtwara (-0.19) and Singida (-0.16) have negative anomalies indicating stress. Rice in bimodal zones mostly faces stable conditions with slight negative or near-zero anomalies across regions such as Singida (-0.08) and Songwe (-0.06). There are no flagged alarming conditions, but coastal and island areas show declining trends for maize and rice in the broader context, not reflected in this data.
  • Malawi: Crop conditions remain overall stable across Malawi's three key crops: beans, maize, and rice. Beans show stable trends with yield anomalies near zero to slightly negative in the Southern Region (-0.04) but positive slight anomalies in Central (0.01) and Northern (0.03) regions. Maize yield anomalies are neutral (around 0.0) across all regions, indicating stable conditions without significant deviation. Rice presents a small negative anomaly in the Southern (-0.03) and Central (-0.04) regions but a positive anomaly in the Northern Region (0.08). There are no alarming trends suggesting poor conditions this month; however, the slight negative anomalies for beans and rice in the south warrant monitoring.
  • Rwanda: For beans, most regions including Iburasirazuba, Amajyaruguru, Iburengerazuba, and Amajyepfo show stable conditions with slightly positive yield anomalies ranging from 0.03 to 0.06 MgT/ha, except Umujyi Wa Kigali which shows a negative anomaly (-0.2). Maize conditions are generally stable but have slightly negative to neutral anomalies from -0.05 to 0.05 with no poor condition flags. Rice shows stable conditions with mostly neutral to slightly positive anomalies (up to 0.06 in Amajyepfo) except small negative anomalies (-0.05) in Amajyaruguru. No alarming declines are observed, but the negative anomaly for beans in Umujyi Wa Kigali stands out as an area to monitor.
  • Zambia: Maize shows mostly favorable conditions across the Northern, Central, Luapula, Copperbelt, Lusaka, Eastern, and North-Western regions with stable to improving trends. However, there is a notable decline in maize conditions in the Southern and Western regions, evidenced by negative yield anomalies of -0.37 and -0.51, respectively, and declining trends. Beans are generally stable with minor negative yield anomalies around -0.02 to -0.01 in Eastern, Southern, Western, and Lusaka regions, with the Northern and Central regions showing small positive anomalies up to 0.04 and 0.01, indicating overall stable conditions without extreme fluctuations. Rice conditions remain largely stable but watchful, with minor negative to near-zero anomalies in most regions except Northern, Luapula and North-Western where slight positive anomalies (up to 0.03) are observed. No severe adverse anomalies are flagged for rice, but the Southern and Western regions exhibit slightly declining or watchful trends.

Production Narrative

Across crops, the largest production areas are for maize in Northern Uganda (1,112,239 ha), which also boasts the highest predicted yield (2.00 MgT/ha) and mean production (approx. 3,137,600 tons), marking it as the top-performing crop-region combination. Maize also performs strongly in Eastern (517,433 ha, 1.57 MgT/ha, ~1,177,828 tons) and Central (229,013 ha, 2.09 MgT/ha, ~679,000 tons) regions. For beans, Northern leads production with 533,916 ha and mean production about 895,718 tons, followed by Eastern and Western regions with moderate yields around 1.18 to 1.26 MgT/ha and production volumes ranging roughly from 248,387 to 141,761 ha. Rice production is much smaller in scale but shows Eastern region as the most productive with a predicted yield of 2.57 MgT/ha over 55,490 ha resulting in about 203,164 tons, followed by Central and Western regions with smaller areas and production volumes. Production trends align with yield anomalies, with maize showing improvement in Northern and Central regions but decline in Western, while bean trends remain stable; rice shows improving trends in Eastern and Central but declining in Western Uganda, underscoring regional disparities and uncertainties in crop outlooks.
