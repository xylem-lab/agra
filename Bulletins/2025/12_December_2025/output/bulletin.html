Overview

This report summarizes yield, acreage, and total production estimates for maize, rice, and beans across focus countries. The data is updated monthly in the RFBS Dashboard via a standard data file. Our model reports on the main growing season for countries with available historical yield data for model training.

Data Limitations and Model Caveats

The data used in this report has limitations due to gaps in historical records and variations in data collection methods:

  • Historical data accuracy and consistency vary by country
  • Our model reflects these data gaps, as it is trained on available information
  • We view these limitations as opportunities to improve data sharing and scaling methods with our country partners

Current Focus: Data presented this month focuses on maize, rice and bean systems in Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania, Malawi, Rwanda, Zambia, which are currently in season.

Executive Summary

Current projections for December 2025 maize, beans, and rice production in East Africa indicate notable variation across estimates from USDA, FAO, and UMD, as summarized in the table and figure below. For maize, Tanzania exhibits the largest discrepancies: USDA and FAO project production between 7.0 and 8.0 million metric tons (MT), whereas UMD reports substantially lower estimates (2.14-4.19M MT), reflecting its narrower geographic coverage. In contrast, Malawi and Zambia show greater agreement across sources, although UMD presents wider maize production ranges (Malawi: 3.77-5.49M MT; Zambia: 2.35-3.18M MT) relative to USDA and FAO estimates. Kenya and Uganda display mixed results. Uganda’s maize production is estimated at 2.8-5.0M MT by USDA/FAO, compared to a more moderate UMD range of 3.12-4.72M MT. For Kenya, UMD projects a broader range (2.71-5.63M MT) than the relatively consistent USDA and FAO estimates of approximately 4.3-4.4M MT. Rice projections generally show lower UMD estimates compared to USDA and FAO, especially in Tanzania (with UMD reporting zero production), Kenya (0.02-0.03M MT versus 4.05-5.18M MT), and Rwanda (0.04-0.06M MT compared to FAO’s 0.13M MT). Bean production estimates are reported only by UMD, indicating regional stability but zero production in Tanzania. Overall, differences among projections largely reflect variations in geographic scope, crop coverage, and sensitivity to seasonal and post-harvest conditions. These results underscore substantial heterogeneity in East African production outlooks, with UMD’s streamlined data approach contributing to lower or more variable estimates for certain crops and countries.

Regional Overview/Conditions

Country Summary

  • Uganda: Bean conditions remain stable across all four regions with positive yield anomalies ranging from +0.334 in Northern to +0.855 in Western regions, indicating generally favorable outcomes. Maize shows mostly stable conditions, with yield anomalies slightly positive across Central (+0.066), Eastern (+0.078), Northern (+0.028), but with a concerning decline trend in the Western region despite a positive anomaly (+0.064). Rice exhibits stable to declining trends: Northern region shows the highest yield anomaly (+0.119) and stable trend, while Central, Eastern, and Western regions have declining trends though anomalies remain slightly positive (+0.048 to +0.139). The declining trend for rice in multiple regions and maize decline in Western Uganda flag a need for monitoring.
  • Kenya: Bean crops are mostly stable or improving in trend across the majority of counties, with some areas like Busia, Nyandarua, Trans Nzoia, and West Pokot showing declining trends. Predicted yield anomalies for beans generally range from slight negatives (e.g., Busia with -0.02) to strong positive anomalies, notably in Garissa (+0.304), Kajiado (+0.321), and Mandera (+0.334), signaling favorable conditions in arid regions. Maize shows a mixed situation: several counties including Bungoma and Migori have improving trends with positive yield anomalies (e.g., Migori +0.303), while others such as Kitui, Makueni, Marsabit, and some arid counties like Wajir and Mandera show declining trends or low yield anomalies. Rice conditions vary mostly between stable and declining trends, with notable declines in counties such as Embu, Kirinyaga, and Nyeri; while counties like Bungoma and Kakamega are improving or stable. The highest positive maize yield anomaly is +0.317 in Kisumu, while negative anomalies are seen in arid counties like Marsabit (-0.002) and Wajir (+0.039) but trending declining.
  • Tanzania: Bean crop conditions exhibit mostly stable to improving trends across regions in both unimodal and bimodal zones. Notably, Iringa and Kigoma show improving trends with yield anomalies of 0.2 and 0.252 MgT/ha respectively, while Mbeya and Mtwara also show improvement. The highest positive bean yield anomaly is 0.377 MgT/ha in Kusini Pemba (bimodal), indicating localized strong performance. However, some regions like Pwani show declining trends. Maize shows a generally stable to improving status with multiple regions such as Katavi (yield anomaly 0.388), Rukwa (0.375), Ruvuma (0.397), and Kigoma (0.389) posting high positive yield anomalies and improving trends, representing favorable conditions. Some zones like Simiyu exhibit a declining trend with a lower anomaly of 0.1. Rice conditions are mostly stable or slightly declining, with coastal and island regions such as Mtwara, Njombe, Singida, and Pwani showing declining or negative trends; anomalies are generally low and range from -0.065 to 0.058 MgT/ha, reflecting limited positive momentum and potential concern for sustaining production. No alarming extremes are noted in the current dataset, but vigilance is needed in coastal zones.
  • Malawi: Crop conditions for December show stable to improving trends across Malawi's key crops. Beans maintain a stable trend in all three regions with slight positive predicted yield anomalies notably in the Northern Region (0.107) and Central Region (0.006), while the Southern Region shows a modest anomaly of 0.028; no alarming negative anomalies detected. Maize exhibits an improving trend with strong positive yield anomalies across all regions: Northern Region leads with 0.506, followed by Southern (0.428) and Central (0.342), reflecting favorable growing conditions. Rice conditions appear stable with minor yield anomalies—positive 0.039 in Central, slightly negative -0.01 in Northern, and -0.006 in Southern Region—indicating no significant risks but some uncertainty especially in Southern rice production due to higher variability reflected in its standard error. Overall, no regions show poor conditions, but the moderate negative anomalies in rice in the North and South warrant monitoring.
  • Rwanda: Bean crop conditions are stable across all five regions, with positive predicted yield anomalies ranging from 0.076 MgT/ha in Amajyepfo to 0.173 MgT/ha in Umujyi Wa Kigali, indicating slightly above-average yields with no poor or watch flags reported. Maize conditions are improving in all four regions with yield anomalies between 0.252 and 0.277 MgT/ha, the highest being in Iburasirazuba (0.277), reflecting a positive trend in performance. Rice conditions show declining trends in four out of five regions with small positive anomalies (0.048) except Umujyi Wa Kigali where the anomaly is 0.008, indicating some concern due to the continuing decline trend despite limited positive yield anomalies. No poor conditions are flagged currently, but the steady decline trend in rice warrants monitoring.
  • Zambia: Beans show generally stable conditions across most regions, with the Southern region being an exception where a declining trend is noted. Predicted yields for beans range from 0.46 MgT/ha in Southern to 0.78 MgT/ha in Northern, with positive yield anomalies evident in all regions (e.g., Northern at 0.121). There are no poor conditions for beans. Maize exhibits mostly stable to improving trends, with positive yield anomalies ranging from 0.184 in Western to 0.478 in Luapula. The highest predicted maize yield is in Central at 2.69 MgT/ha, and no region shows declining trends for maize, although Southern has the lowest yield of 1.47 MgT/ha and stable trend. Rice conditions show some decline in certain regions such as Central and Southern where predicted yield anomalies are slightly negative (e.g., Central -0.064) and trends are declining, while other regions like Eastern and North-Western remain stable. Rice yields vary from 0.66 MgT/ha in Lusaka (declining trend) to 1.42 MgT/ha in Luapula (stable trend). Overall, the Southern region demonstrates concerning declines in beans and rice, while Western also shows a declining trend in beans and rice.

Production Narrative

Total production area for beans ranges from 109,934 ha in Central to 533,916 ha in Northern regions. Top bean production is forecast for Northern Uganda with mean predicted production of 530,891 tons, followed by Eastern (230,148 tons) and Western (192,444 tons). Maize shows the largest production area in Northern (1,112,239 ha), with predicted mean production of 2,337,455 tons, ranking it the top-performing crop overall. Eastern (781,790 tons) and Central (551,254 tons) follow in maize production. Western maize shows a declining trend despite 295,313 ha area and moderate mean production (246,974 tons). Rice production area is much smaller; Northern leads with 119,279 ha and mean 166,501 tons, followed by Eastern (61,582 tons) and Western (28,685 tons), though rice trends are declining in all but Northern Uganda. Production uncertainties are noted mainly in Western maize and rice, reflected by declining trends and relatively higher standard errors in predicted yields.

The highest positive maize yield anomaly is +0.317 in Kisumu, while negative anomalies are seen in arid counties like Marsabit (-0.002) and Wajir (+0.039) but trending declining.
