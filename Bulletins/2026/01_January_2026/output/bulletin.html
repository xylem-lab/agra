Bulletin Overview

Overview

This report summarizes yield, acreage, and total production estimates for Maize, Beans, Rice across focus countries. The data is updated monthly in the RFBS Dashboard via a standard data file. Our model reports on the main growing season for countries with available historical yield data for model training.

Data Limitations and Model Caveats

The data used in this report has limitations due to gaps in historical records and variations in data collection methods:

  • Historical data accuracy and consistency vary by country
  • Our model reflects these data gaps, as it is trained on available information
  • We view these limitations as opportunities to improve data sharing and scaling methods with our country partners

Current Focus: Data presented this month covers Maize, Beans, Rice systems in Kenya, Malawi, Zambia, United Republic Of Tanzania, which are currently in season.

Note on excluded countries: Uganda, Rwanda are not included in this month's bulletin. These countries are currently post-harvest or out of the growing season — once harvesting begins, vegetation indices (NDVI/EVI) are no longer reliable predictors of crop yield and model forecasts are suspended until the next season commences.

Tanzania — Vuli season not covered: This bulletin reports Tanzania's Msimu season only. The Vuli season is currently out of season for January — crops are either pre-planting or post-harvest and satellite-based yield forecasts are not applicable. The Vuli season will be covered in a future bulletin once it becomes active.

Summary

Executive Summary

Current projections for January 2026 across Kenya, Malawi, Tanzania, and Zambia show significant variance between USDA/FAO global estimates and the UMD-XylemLab regional models for Maize, Rice, and Beans production (see summarized Table and Figure below). For maize, USDA and FAO estimates align closely in Malawi and Zambia, with production around 3.0–3.5 million MT in Malawi and approximately 3.3–3.4 million MT in Zambia, while UMD-XylemLab projects wider ranges, indicating higher uncertainty (e.g., Malawi 2.37–5.42 million MT, Zambia 2.42–5.26 million MT). In Kenya, UMD estimates are notably lower (0.19–0.65 million MT) compared to USDA/FAO (4.28–4.4 million MT), reflecting its narrower area scope, while Tanzania’s maize production from UMD ranges between 0.86 and 2.05 million MT in the absence of USDA or FAO data. Rice production forecasts show USDA and FAO figures for Kenya near 4.05–5.8 million MT, whereas UMD reports negligible to zero output. Malawi and Zambia rice estimates from FAO are low (0.006–0.12 million MT), with UMD ranges overlapping but slightly lower. Tanzania rice production by UMD is moderate (0.14–0.43 million MT). Beans estimates from UMD reflect modest production across Malawi, Kenya, Tanzania, and Zambia ranging from 0.03 to 0.46 million MT; USDA and FAO data for beans are not present for comparison. Variability across models stems largely from differences in geographic coverage, seasonal growth stages, and data inputs. Overall, the projections depict stable to somewhat uncertain production levels across crops and countries, with UMD-XylemLab providing broader uncertainty ranges and lower area estimates in key regions.

While rice conditions remain stable, there are significant negative yield anomalies in Embu (-8.39%) and Tharaka-Nithi (-8.73%), with predicted yields between 0.53 and 1.67 Mg/ha across regions. These localized negative anomalies suggest production stress in specific rice-growing areas despite the overall regional stability.
  • Malawi: Crop conditions remain stable across Malawi's three major crops: beans, maize, and rice. All regions report stable trends for beans, with predicted yield anomalies near zero (0.0) and mild positive percentage anomalies around 0.6%. Rice exhibits the largest negative yield anomalies, particularly in Central (-0.16, -10.37%) and Northern (-0.14, -9.73%) regions; while the trend is classified as stable, these figures warrant close monitoring. While no alarming severity in conditions is detected, rice shows the largest negative anomalies, especially in Luapula (-0.13) and Eastern (-0.10).

Kenya

Kenya is currently in the short rains season (October to March), with the planting period ongoing and harvest expected to start in February and extend through March. This period marks the final stages before harvest for maize, beans, and rice crops, with production forecasts becoming clearer as the season progresses.

Yield anomalies range modestly from 1.0% to 1.5% positive but remain stable, with predicted yields varying from 0.23 to 0.66 Mg/ha; the highest being in Tharaka-Nithi and the lowest in Samburu. Maize conditions are stable overall but exhibit low to moderate negative yield anomalies in arid and semi-arid counties, such as Garissa (-1.14%) and Marsabit (-2.25%). Predicted maize yields range widely from 0.29 Mg/ha in Mandera to as high as 1.72 Mg/ha in Samburu. Beans Maps

Regional Beans Yield Forecasts

In January 2026, Tharaka-Nithi led Kenya's bean yield prediction with 0.66 MgT/ha, while Samburu reported the lowest yield at 0.23 MgT/ha. Regional yields ranged from 0.23 to 0.66 MgT/ha, highlighting strong production in central regions versus weaker output in the north.

Regional Beans Yield Anomaly

In January 2026, key bean-growing regions in Kenya showed varying yield anomalies, with Kitui, Machakos, and Tharaka-Nithi recording the highest positive anomalies around 0.01 MgT/ha. Coversely, Makueni and Embu exhibited the most significant negative anomalies near 0.00 MgT/ha. Large areas lack data for this period, limiting the overall anomaly assessment.

Rice Maps

Regional Rice Yield Forecasts

In January 2026, rice yields in Kenya were highest in Garissa at 1.67 MgT/ha, while Embu recorded the lowest yield of 0.53 MgT/ha, which indicated a significant regional disparity, with Embu experiencing notably below-average production during this period.

Regional Rice Yield Anomaly

In January 2026, the highest rice yield anomaly was reported in Embu, with a negative anomaly of -0.05 MgT/ha, while Meru and Tharaka-Nithi experienced the largest negative anomalies at -0.10 MgT/ha. These figures indicated notably below-average yields in these regions. Garissa also showed a moderate negative anomaly of -0.08 MgT/ha, with no data available for most other counties during this period.

Production Narrative

Beans were predicted to produce significant volumes especially in Kitui (approximately 66,452 tons), Machakos (31,360 tons), and Makueni (27,213 tons) based on their large cultivation areas and stable yields. Maize production was highest in Kitui with a mean predicted production of 97,301 tons, followed by Machakos with 67,905 tons and Meru with 69,632 tons, confirming maize as the top-producing crop in terms of volume. Rice production was considerably lower in scale, with the highest output recorded in Meru (1,746 tons), Embu (266 tons), and Tharaka-Nithi (459 tons). While production trends for all crops remained stable, rice exhibited notable uncertainties and negative anomalies that may affect final outcomes in some regions. Overall, maize led in total production volume, followed by beans and then rice, reflecting the importance of maize and beans for food security in the bimodal rainfall regions of Kenya.

Malawi

Figure: Agricultural Calendar – Malawi

Malawi is currently in the main growing season that started in November 2025 and extends through July 2026. The main planting window is concluding, with harvesting scheduled to begin from April 2026 and continue through July 2026.

Regional Crop Conditions Summary

Crop conditions remain stable across Malawi's three major crops: beans, maize, and rice. All regions report stable trends for beans, with predicted yield anomalies near zero (0.0) and mild positive percentage anomalies around 0.6%. Maize shows slight negative yield anomalies across regions, ranging from -0.03 in the Central region to nearly neutral (-0.00) in the South, though overall trends remain stable. Rice exhibits the largest negative yield anomalies, particularly in Central (-0.16, -10.37%) and Northern (-0.14, -9.73%) regions; while the trend is classified as stable, these figures warrant close monitoring. There are no alarming conditions reported for beans or maize, but the localized rice anomalies in the Central is a point of concern. The highest predicted yield extremes are recorded for beans in the Southern Region (max 1.59 Mg/ha) and maize in the Central Region (max 3.46 Mg/ha).

Production Narrative

Beans covers about 367,447 hectares across the three regions, with the highest predicted mean production recorded in the Southern Region at approximately 100,038 tons, followed by Central Region at 92,483 tons and Northern Region at 45,570 tons. Maize cultivation occupies the largest area at approximately 1,827,038 hectares; the Central Region led in mean predicted production at about 2,145,956 tons, Southern Region at 864,555 tons, and Northern Region at 719,744 tons. Rice is grown on smaller areas (~79,059 hectares combined), with Central Region showing the highest mean production forecast of 53,734 tons, followed by Southern at 32,694 tons and Northern at 19,419 tons. The primary uncertainty resides in rice production, as indicated by its larger negative yield anomalies and higher variability in maximum production estimates, whereas beans and maize production estimates maintain narrower ranges and stable trends.

Zambia

Regional Crop Conditions Summary

Beans show stable conditions across all 11 provinces with no negative predicted yield anomalies; yields range from 0.46 MgT/ha in the Southern province to 0.67 MgT/ha in the North-Western province. Maize also depicts stability with minor negative yield anomalies from -0.01 to -0.03, with yields varying between 1.32 MgT/ha in Western and 3.39 MgT/ha in Muchinga. Rice exhibits generally stable but watchful conditions, characterized by negative yield anomalies from -0.07 to -0.13; yields are lowest in Lusaka at 0.8 MgT/ha and highest in Muchinga at 1.45 MgT/ha. While no alarming severity in conditions is detected, rice shows the largest negative anomalies, especially in Luapula (-0.13) and Eastern (-0.10).

Model Performance Summary

Our yield prediction model is evaluated using historical data to assess accuracy and reliability. The ensemble approach combines multiple remote sensing inputs (NDVI, ESI, precipitation, soil moisture) across a random forest architecture trained per country and crop.

Metrics Explanation

  • R² Score: Coefficient of determination (0–1); values above 0.7 indicate strong predictive power.
  • RMSE: Root Mean Square Error — average magnitude of prediction errors in MT/ha. Lower is better.
  • MAE: Mean Absolute Error — average absolute difference between predicted and actual yields in MT/ha. Lower is better.
