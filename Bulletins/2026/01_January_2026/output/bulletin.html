Bulletin Overview

Overview

This report summarizes yield, acreage, and total production estimates for Maize, Beans, Rice across focus countries. The data is updated monthly in the RFBS Dashboard via a standard data file. Our model reports on the main growing season for countries with available historical yield data for model training.

Data Limitations and Model Caveats

The data used in this report has limitations due to gaps in historical records and variations in data collection methods:

  • Historical data accuracy and consistency vary by country
  • Our model reflects these data gaps, as it is trained on available information
  • We view these limitations as opportunities to improve data sharing and scaling methods with our country partners

Current Focus: Data presented this month covers Maize, Beans, Rice systems in Kenya, United Republic Of Tanzania, Malawi, Zambia, which are currently in season.

Note on excluded countries: Uganda, Rwanda are not included in this month's bulletin. These countries are currently post-harvest or out of the growing season — once harvesting begins, vegetation indices (NDVI/EVI) are no longer reliable predictors of crop yield and model forecasts are suspended until the next season commences.

Tanzania — Vuli season not covered: This bulletin reports Tanzania's Msimu season only. The Vuli season is currently out of season for January — crops are either pre-planting or post-harvest and satellite-based yield forecasts are not applicable. The Vuli season will be covered in a future bulletin once it becomes active.

Summary

Executive Summary

Current projections for January 2026 Beans, Maize, Rice production in Kenya, Malawi, United Republic Of Tanzania, Zambia show varying forecasts between USDA, FAO and UMD-XylemLab models, as summarized in Table and Figure below. For maize, Malawi and Zambia exhibit close USDA and FAO estimates, with Malawi ranging from 3.0 to 3.5 million MT and Zambia near 3.3 to 3.4 million MT, while UMD-XylemLab offers higher and broader ranges, reflecting uncertainty and including Malawi's 2.74 to 4.71 million MT and Zambia’s 3.0 to 4.47 million MT. Kenya’s maize production shows a marked difference with USDA and FAO estimates around 4.3 to 4.4 million MT, contrasting with a substantially lower UMD estimate (0.05 to 0.8 million MT), likely due to UMD’s narrower area scope. UMD-XylemLab is the sole source providing projections for Tanzania, indicating maize production between 0.83 and 2.03 million MT. Rice forecasts show FAA and USDA values in Malawi and Kenya exceeding UMD estimates; for example, Kenya's 4.05 to 4.1 million MT (USDA/FAO) versus near zero by UMD. Beans data is only from UMD-XylemLab, signaling moderate production with ranges such as 0.12 to 0.39 million MT in Malawi and 0.04 to 0.16 million MT in Tanzania. Discrepancies reflect differences in geographic coverage and model sensitivity to seasonal transitions. Overall, the production landscape for January 2026 highlights stability in maize and beans with notable uncertainty in rice and Kenyan maize projections across sources.

Regional Overview/Conditions

Country Summary

  • Kenya: Bean crops across Kenya show a predominantly stable trend with all reported regions reflecting stable conditions. Yield anomalies for beans are mostly small positive, with Samburu showing a notably high anomaly of 211.43% indicating localized improving conditions. Maize conditions are mixed with some regions displaying slight negative yield anomalies, but overall trends remain stable. Notably, Garissa, Isiolo, and Kitui show negative yield anomalies ranging from -1.37% to -3.63%. Rice conditions are stable but show negative yield anomalies in all recorded counties, with values ranging from -3.84% in Garissa to -15.93% in Embu, indicating some concerns in rice productivity. No extreme poor statuses are flagged but the negative anomalies in rice warrant attention.
  • United Republic Of Tanzania: Crop conditions across Tanzania's regions for beans, maize, and rice remain generally stable, with no alarming trends observed. Beans show stable trends with positive yield anomalies mostly under 1.5%, indicating near-normal conditions. Maize exhibits negative yield anomalies across all regions, ranging from -0.7% to -3.5%, signaling some stress but still stable trends. Rice is facing stronger negative yield anomalies, between -4% and nearly -11%, particularly in coastal regions like Mtwara and Lindi. No regions report poor conditions outright, but rice's negative anomalies warrant close monitoring.
  • Malawi: Crop conditions in Malawi remain stable across beans, maize, and rice. Beans show positive yield anomalies in the Central (1.09%) and Northern Regions (0.82%), with a smaller positive anomaly in the Southern Region (0.31%), indicating generally favorable conditions with no alarming trends. Maize yields are stable but show slight negative anomalies across all regions: Central (-0.64%), Northern (-0.39%), and Southern (-0.35%), signalling a slight downward pressure but overall stable conditions. Rice exhibits the most notable negative anomalies, with Central Region at -6.57%, Northern at -7.82%, and Southern at -8.48%, suggesting less favorable conditions and greater cause for monitoring. No crop is currently flagged as poor or watch, but rice requires attention due to consistent and larger negative anomalies.
  • Zambia: For beans, all regions indicate stable trends with only minor positive yield anomalies ranging roughly from 0.6% to 1.3%, reflecting generally stable and favorable conditions without alarming deviations. The predicted bean yields range between 0.46 to 0.67 Mg/ha, with North-Western and Lusaka showing the highest and lowest extremes respectively. Maize shows stable conditions across all surveyed regions but with small negative yield anomalies between -0.25% and -0.62%, pointing to slight but consistent decreases in expected yields, notably in Southern and Western regions where predicted yields are on the lower side (1.32 to 2.35 Mg/ha), signaling mild concern. Rice conditions are stable but with uniformly negative yield anomalies from about -5.4% in Muchinga to -11.6% in Lusaka, which highlights a cautious outlook. Predicted rice yields range from 0.75 to 1.49 Mg/ha, with Eastern and Lusaka provinces presenting the lowest and highest extremes respectively. No severe deteriorations are evident but rice yields warrant continued monitoring due to the consistent negative anomalies.

In January 2026, Meru shows the highest positive maize yield anomaly at +0.01 MgT/ha, while Wajir records the lowest at -0.02 MgT/ha, indicating slight above-average yields in Meru and below-average yields especially in Wajir and adjacent regions.

Beans Maps

Regional Beans Yield Forecasts

Most eastern regions including Wajir (0.46 MgT/ha) and Mandera (0.30 MgT/ha) display moderate yields, with large areas lacking data.

Regional Beans Yield Anomaly

In January 2026, Meru and Embu regions showed the highest positive beans yield anomalies at 0.02 MgT/ha, while Tharaka-Nithi experienced a lower anomaly around 0.01 MgT/ha, indicating a notable yield dip. Most other labeled regions exhibit small positive anomalies near 0.01–0.02 MgT/ha, with many areas lacking data.

Rice Maps

Regional Rice Yield Forecasts

In January 2026, Garissa is predicted to have the highest rice yield at 1.68 MgT/ha, while Embu shows the lowest yield at 0.49 MgT/ha, indicating notable regional variation across Kenya. Meru also performs well above average at 1.20 MgT/ha, whereas most other regions have no data available.

Regional Rice Yield Anomaly

In January 2026, Tharaka-Nithi recorded the largest negative rice yield anomaly in Kenya at -0.10 MgT/ha, followed by Meru and Embu with anomalies of -0.09 MgT/ha, indicating below-average yields in these regions.

Production Narrative

Beans cover a total reported area of approximately 366,036 ha across key counties. Kitui leads bean production with a mean predicted production of 47,846 tons, followed by Machakos (29,584 tons) and Makueni (16,630 tons). Maize is the top-performing crop in terms of production volume with large areas in Kitui (262,977 ha) and Machakos (117,077 ha) yielding mean predicted productions of 63,114 and 73,759 tons respectively; Meru also provides a substantial maize output (68,452 tons). Rice occupies smaller areas with lower production figures; Meru has the highest mean predicted rice production at 1,776 tons, with Embu and Garissa producing 245 and 32 tons respectively. While trends are generally stable for all crops, maize shows some slight negative yield anomalies in several counties, suggesting localized production uncertainties to monitor in upcoming reports.

Production Narrative

The total area under beans is around 165,000 hectares cumulatively across regions, with notable mean predicted productions including Tabora (19,429 tons), Dodoma (13,162 tons), and Singida (12,793 tons). Maize has the largest cultivation area, exceeding 900,000 hectares combined, with highest mean predicted productions in Tabora (317,837 tons), Rukwa (222,291 tons), and Mbeya (167,864 tons); however, maize shows consistent negative yield anomalies indicating some yield decrease risk. Rice production areas cover smaller extents, with key production in Rukwa (47,618 tons), Mbeya (32,336 tons), and Tabora (61,371 tons), but rice faces the highest negative yield anomalies and greater uncertainty. Overall, maize remains the top-producing crop by volume despite slight yield setbacks, followed by rice and beans, with stable trends but varying regional performances.

Crop conditions in Malawi remain stable across beans, maize, and rice. Predicted rice yields range from 0.75 to 1.49 Mg/ha, with Eastern and Lusaka provinces presenting the lowest and highest extremes respectively. No severe deteriorations are evident but rice yields warrant continued monitoring due to the consistent negative anomalies.

Production Narrative

Maize dominates with the largest production area across all regions totaling approximately 1,097,220 hectares cumulatively (sum of individual region areas) and predicted mean production exceeding 3.4 million tons. Top maize producing regions include Central (mean production ~1,029,439 tons), Eastern (~756,964 tons), and Southern (~604,636 tons) provinces. Beans have more modest production areas, with Central (~32,002 ha), Eastern (~33,990 ha), and Southern (~23,915 ha) as leading areas and mean predicted productions ranging from about 4,460 to 17,281 tons regionally, indicating steady but smaller-scale production relative to maize. Rice has the smallest cultivation area, ranging from 334 ha in Western to 12,739 ha in Eastern, with mean production estimates generally below 12,114 tons, reflecting a minor share of total crop output. Trends for maize and beans are stable, albeit with slight negative yield anomalies for maize and stable positive anomalies for beans, whereas rice shows uniform negative yield anomalies, suggesting potential risks for rice production stability.

Model Performance Summary

Our yield prediction model is evaluated using historical data to assess accuracy and reliability. The ensemble approach combines multiple remote sensing inputs (NDVI, ESI, precipitation, soil moisture) across a random forest architecture trained per country and crop.

Metrics Explanation

  • R² Score: Coefficient of determination (0–1); values above 0.7 indicate strong predictive power.
  • RMSE: Root Mean Square Error — average magnitude of prediction errors in MT/ha. Lower is better.
  • MAE: Mean Absolute Error — average absolute difference between predicted and actual yields in MT/ha. Lower is better.
