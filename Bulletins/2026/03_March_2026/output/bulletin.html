Bulletin Overview

Overview

This report summarizes yield, acreage, and total production estimates for Maize, Rice, Beans across focus countries. The data is updated monthly in the RFBS Dashboard via a standard data file. Our model reports on the main growing season for countries with available historical yield data for model training.

Data Limitations and Model Caveats

The data used in this report has limitations due to gaps in historical records and variations in data collection methods:

  • Historical data accuracy and consistency vary by country
  • Our model reflects these data gaps, as it is trained on available information
  • We view these limitations as opportunities to improve data sharing and scaling methods with our country partners

Current Focus: Data presented this month covers Maize, Rice, Beans systems in Uganda, Rwanda, Kenya, Malawi, Zambia, United Republic Of Tanzania, which are currently in season.

Tanzania — Vuli season not covered: This bulletin reports Tanzania's Msimu season only. The Vuli season is currently out of season for March — crops are either pre-planting or post-harvest and satellite-based yield forecasts are not applicable. The Vuli season will be covered in a future bulletin once it becomes active.

Summary

Executive Summary

Current projections for March 2026 Beans, Maize, Rice production in Kenya, Malawi, Rwanda, Uganda, United Republic Of Tanzania, and Zambia show varying forecasts between USDA, FAO and UMD-XylemLab models, as summarized in Table and Figure below. For maize, USDA and FAO estimates are comparatively close in Malawi (around 3.0–3.5 million MT) and Zambia (about 3.3–3.4 million MT), while UMD-XylemLab shows wider ranges with higher upper bounds, such as Malawi maize between 2.42 and 5.38 million MT and Zambia between 2.43 and 5.21 million MT. Kenya’s maize production reveals USDA and FAO estimates near 4.3–4.4 million MT, but UMD indicates a broader range from 2.88 to 6.74 million MT. Uganda’s maize projections vary widely, with USDA and UMD ranges overlapping broadly (2.76–5.68 million MT by UMD vs. 5.0 million MT by USDA), but FAO displays a lower area estimate resulting in a smaller total (2.8 million MT). For rice, FAO reports stable production in Malawi, Rwanda, Kenya, Uganda, and Zambia, generally around 0.1 to 5.0 million MT, whereas UMD-XylemLab shows notably lower figures, especially in Kenya (0.01–0.05 million MT) and Zambia (0.02–0.08 million MT), reflecting model sensitivity to regional cropping patterns. Beans are reported only by UMD-XylemLab across all six countries, showing moderate production with variable ranges, such as 0.42–1.8 million MT in Kenya and 1.08–4.31 million MT in Uganda. Differences across models largely stem from geographic scope, seasonal transitions, and sensitivity to subnational factors. Overall, these projections highlight substantial variability driven by methodological differences and local conditions, emphasizing the need for integrated approaches to refine production forecasts across the region.

Regional Overview/Conditions

Country Summary

  • Uganda: Across Uganda's four regions, crop conditions remain generally stable for beans, maize, and rice in the March–July season. Beans show stable trends with slight positive yield anomalies around 1.5% in Central, Eastern, and Northern regions, reaching a peak predicted yield of 3.14 MgT/ha in the Northern region. Maize conditions are also stable but exhibit small negative yield anomalies (up to -0.36%) in Northern and Western regions, though yields remain relatively strong especially in Eastern (2.82 MgT/ha) and Western (2.9 MgT/ha) areas. Rice presents mixed signals: the Western region stands out with a positive anomaly of +9.64%, and highest predicted yield of 1.64 MgT/ha, contrasting with negative anomalies nearing -5.57% in the Northern region. No alarming deviations are observed, but the variability in rice yield anomalies warrants continued monitoring.
  • Rwanda: Crop conditions remain stable overall in March 2026. For beans, all five regions (Amajyaruguru, Amajyepfo, Iburasirazuba, Iburengerazuba, and Umujyi Wa Kigali) show stable trends with yield anomalies close to zero ranging from -0.42% in Iburengerazuba to +0.48% in Iburasirazuba. No poor conditions are flagged, but Umujyi Wa Kigali stands out with the highest predicted yield of 1.69 MgT/ha. Maize is stable with slight negative yield anomalies from -0.55% in Amajyaruguru to -1.01% in Umujyi Wa Kigali; all regions show consistent conditions without poor flags. Rice shows more regional variation: stable in Iburengerazuba with a positive anomaly of +10.6%, but negative anomalies in Iburasirazuba (-8.24%), Amajyepfo (-3.56%), and Umujyi Wa Kigali (-8.45%). The notable positive anomaly and highest predicted yield for rice is in Iburengerazuba at 5.61 MgT/ha. Outside of rice in Iburengerazuba, no alarming declines or poor condition flags are present this season.
  • Kenya: Bean crops are largely stable across all counties with mostly neutral predicted yield anomalies close to zero, indicating overall steady performance with minor positive deviations up to +0.8% in some areas like Kajiado. No alarming deviations are observed, but the variability in rice yield anomalies warrants continued monitoring.

Production Narrative

Beans cover areas ranging from approximately 110,000 hectares in Central to over 530,000 hectares in Northern Uganda, with mean predicted production peaking at about 1.68 million tons in the Northern region. Maize occupies significant cultivation zones, largest in Northern Uganda at over 1.1 million hectares, generating forecasted productions around 1.4 million tons, with Eastern and Western regions producing between 856,000 and 1.46 million tons respectively. Rice is cultivated on smaller areas but with notable production levels; Eastern and Northern regions have mean predicted production of about 90,000 and 113,000 tons respectively, with Western Uganda showing nearly 52,000 tons forecast. Beans lead as the top-performing crop in production volume due to their extensive area and high predicted yields in Northern Uganda. Trends across all crops and regions are stable, with standard errors indicating moderate uncertainty especially in bean yields in Northern Uganda (std error 2.26).

Model Performance Summary

Our yield prediction model is evaluated using historical data to assess accuracy and reliability. Performance metrics are calculated by comparing predicted yields against actual reported yields from previous seasons. The ensemble approach combines multiple remote sensing inputs (NDVI, ESI, precipitation, soil moisture) across a random forest architecture trained per country and crop.

Metrics Explanation

  • R² Score: Coefficient of determination (0–1); values above 0.7 indicate strong predictive power.
  • RMSE: Root Mean Square Error — average magnitude of prediction errors in MT/ha. Lower is better.
  • MAE: Mean Absolute Error — average absolute difference between predicted and actual yields in MT/ha. Lower is better.
