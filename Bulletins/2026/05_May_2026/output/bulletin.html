Bulletin Overview

Overview

This report summarizes yield, acreage, and total production estimates for Maize, Rice, Beans across focus countries. The data is updated monthly in the RFBS Dashboard via a standard data file. Our model reports on the main growing season for countries with available historical yield data for model training.

Data Limitations and Model Caveats

The data used in this report has limitations due to gaps in historical records and variations in data collection methods:

  • Historical data accuracy and consistency vary by country
  • Our model reflects these data gaps, as it is trained on available information
  • We view these limitations as opportunities to improve data sharing and scaling methods with our country partners

Current Focus: Data presented this month covers Maize, Rice, Beans systems in Uganda, Rwanda, Malawi, United Republic Of Tanzania, Kenya, which are currently in season.

Note on excluded countries: Zambia is not included in this month's bulletin. This country is currently post-harvest or out of the growing season — once harvesting begins, vegetation indices (NDVI/EVI) are no longer reliable predictors of crop yield and model forecasts are suspended until the next season commences.

Summary

Executive Summary

Current projections for May 2026 Beans, Maize, Rice production in Kenya, Malawi, Rwanda, Uganda, United Republic Of Tanzania show varying forecasts between USDA, FAO and UMD-XylemLab models, as summarized in Table and Figure below. For maize, UMD-XylemLab generally presents wider production ranges reflecting regional variability, such as Malawi's 2.34–5.67 million MT and Kenya's 2.89–6.75 million MT, compared to USDA and FAO averages that are more conservative (e.g., Malawi at 3.0–3.5 million MT, Kenya at 4.28–4.4 million MT). Uganda shows USDA and UMD estimates closer in magnitude (around 2.8–5.0 million MT) while FAO is markedly lower at 2.8 million MT with a smaller area reported. Rice forecasts indicate that FAO and USDA estimates for Kenya (around 4.05–5.18 million MT) exceed UMD's much lower range (0.01–0.05 million MT), likely due to differing geographic focus and yield assumptions. Beans predictions by UMD-XylemLab display considerable variability across countries, with Kenya ranging from 0.43 to 1.8 million MT and Uganda from 1.1 to 4.24 million MT, emphasizing uncertainty in area and yield estimates. Tanzania's UMD-only data shows modest maize and rice productions with narrow ranges, reflecting a partial geographic scope. Overall, discrepancies across models highlight the impact of geographic scope, seasonal dynamics, and methodological differences on production forecasts for May 2026 in these East African countries.

Maize also exhibits stable conditions in all regions, with small negative yield anomalies ranging from -0.01 to -0.75 percent. Rice conditions are stable but show modest negative yield anomalies, especially in Amajyepfo (-6.53%), Iburasirazuba (-8.09%), Umujyi Wa Kigali (-8.49%), and Iburengerazuba (-2.36%), signaling slight but notable yield reductions in rice. No regions report poor conditions, but the negative rice anomalies warrant monitoring.
  • Malawi: Crop conditions are stable across Malawi for beans, maize, and rice in May 2026. Beans show stable trends with yield anomalies close to zero in the Central (0.0) and Southern Regions (0.0), and a slight positive anomaly in the Northern Region (0.01). Maize exhibits small positive yield anomalies between 0.03 and 0.04 across all regions, indicating generally favorable and stable conditions. Rice shows higher positive yield anomalies ranging from 0.19 to 0.26, especially notable in the Southern Region (0.26), signaling favorable conditions but with higher variability as reflected by larger standard errors. No regions report poor conditions; the yields and anomalies confirm stable and favorable crop health.
  • United Republic Of Tanzania: Crop conditions for beans, maize, and rice in the bimodal regions of Tanzania in May 2026 are generally stable with no major anomalies reported. Beans exhibit stable trends across all monitored regions with predicted yield anomalies close to zero or slightly positive, such as Mwanza and Shinyanga with yields around 0.93 to 1.12 Mg/ha. Maize similarly shows stable conditions with mostly neutral to very small negative anomalies, except Dar Es Salaam region where a slight negative anomaly (-0.51%) is observed; yields are highest in Kilimanjaro (2.04 Mg/ha) and Geita (1.91 Mg/ha). Rice shows a mixed pattern with some coastal and island regions experiencing negative anomalies up to -8.31% in Dar Es Salaam and Kusini Unguja (-6.99%), while Kagera and Mara regions show small positive anomalies close to 9.9%. No alarming conditions are flagged, but variability is notable in rice yields particularly in coastal zones.
  • Kenya: Bean crop conditions are generally stable with most regions showing neutral or slight positive yield anomalies around zero and under a stable trend. No alarming negative anomalies exist, although yields vary significantly across counties, ranging from as low as 0.14 Mg/ha in Marsabit to 1.36 Mg/ha in Baringo. Maize shows more variability, with many regions experiencing small negative yield anomalies typically around -0.01 to -0.02 Mg/ha and slight declines in yield anomalies, signaling some production stress especially in arid and semi-arid counties such as Mandera, Marsabit, and Garissa. However, several Rift Valley and western counties like Trans Nzoia and Uasin Gishu report relatively high predicted yields above 4 Mg/ha and stable trends. Rice conditions are stable but with notable negative yield anomalies across regions typically between -0.03 and -0.31 Mg/ha, the largest being in Kirinyaga (-0.31). The trend for rice also remains stable. Overall, beans present the most stable conditions, maize the widest range with moderate declines in some areas, and rice consistent but with reduced yields compared to historical averages.

No crop region is currently flagged as poor; beans and maize have stable outlooks while rice requires monitoring in eastern and northern parts due to mild negative anomalies.

Overall, maize dominates as the top-performing crop by production volume, followed by beans and then rice.

Model Performance Summary

Our yield prediction model is evaluated using historical data to assess accuracy and reliability. Performance metrics are calculated by comparing predicted yields against actual reported yields from previous seasons. The ensemble approach combines multiple remote sensing inputs (NDVI, ESI, precipitation, soil moisture) across a random forest architecture trained per country and crop.

Metrics Explanation

  • R² Score: Coefficient of determination (0–1); values above 0.7 indicate strong predictive power.
  • RMSE: Root Mean Square Error — average magnitude of prediction errors in MT/ha. Lower is better.
  • MAE: Mean Absolute Error — average absolute difference between predicted and actual yields in MT/ha. Lower is better.
