Bulletin Overview

Overview

This report summarizes yield, acreage, and total production estimates for Maize, Beans, Rice across focus countries. The data is updated monthly in the RFBS Dashboard via a standard data file. Our model reports on the main growing season for countries with available historical yield data for model training.

Data Limitations and Model Caveats

The data used in this report has limitations due to gaps in historical records and variations in data collection methods:

  • Historical data accuracy and consistency vary by country
  • Our model reflects these data gaps, as it is trained on available information
  • We view these limitations as opportunities to improve data sharing and scaling methods with our country partners

Current Focus: Data presented this month covers Maize, Beans, Rice systems in United Republic Of Tanzania, Malawi, Kenya, which are currently in season.

Note on excluded countries: Uganda, Rwanda, Zambia are not included in this month's bulletin. These countries are currently post-harvest or out of the growing season — once harvesting begins, vegetation indices (NDVI/EVI) are no longer reliable predictors of crop yield and model forecasts are suspended until the next season commences.

Summary

Executive Summary

Current projections for June 2026 Beans, Maize, Rice production in Kenya, Malawi, United Republic Of Tanzania show varying forecasts between USDA, FAO and UMD-XylemLab models, as summarized in Table and Figure below. In Malawi, maize production estimates vary, with USDA reporting 3.0 million MT, FAO slightly higher at 3.5 million MT, and UMD-XylemLab indicating a wider range from 2.3 to 5.77 million MT, reflecting potential yield improvements captured by satellite data. Rice forecasts in Malawi show lower values, with FAO at 0.12 million MT and UMD-XylemLab providing a range between 0.06 and 0.26 million MT, indicating some uncertainty likely due to planting area differences. Beans data is only available from UMD-XylemLab with a range of 0.11 to 0.45 million MT. For Kenya, maize projections show USDA and FAO closely aligned around 4.4 and 4.28 million MT respectively, while UMD-XylemLab presents a broader range of 2.86 to 6.76 million MT, suggesting variability in regional yield estimates. Rice production in Kenya contrasts sharply between USDA/FAO estimates near 4.1 and 4.05 million MT and a significantly lower UMD-XylemLab range of 0.01 to 0.05 million MT, likely due to its narrower geographical scope or differing assessment methodologies. Beans estimates from UMD-XylemLab range between 0.43 and 1.81 million MT. In the United Republic Of Tanzania, only UMD-XylemLab data is available, showing maize production ranging from 0.75 to 1.81 million MT, rice between 0.16 and 0.53 million MT, and beans from 0.05 to 0.18 million MT, reflecting the model's focus on specific regions and possible seasonal factors. Overall, these June 2026 forecasts highlight significant discrepancies between data sources often driven by geographic scope and methodological differences, underscoring the importance of multi-source interpretation for robust production assessments.

Regional Overview/Conditions

Country Summary

  • United Republic Of Tanzania: Crop conditions for beans, maize, and rice remain generally stable across the bimodal regions with no alarming negative yield anomalies reported. Beans show stable trends with predicted yield anomalies near zero (ranging from 0.0 to 0.01) across regions such as Arusha, Kilimanjaro, and Shinyanga. Maize yields are also stable, with predicted yield anomalies close to zero or slightly positive in many regions including Arusha (0.01), Geita (0.01), and Manyara (0.01), though Dar Es Salaam and some island areas like Kusini Unguja show slightly negative anomalies (-0.36 and -0.36 respectively). Rice presents a mixed pattern: some coastal and island regions like Arusha, Dar Es Salaam, and Kilimanjaro exhibit negative yield anomalies (down to -0.21) while regions such as Kagera and Tanga have small positive anomalies (up to 0.11). Overall, no extensive poor conditions are evident, but coastal and island areas show somewhat lower rice yields, consistent with observed stable to slightly declining trends.
  • Malawi: Crop conditions across Malawi in 2026 remain stable for beans, maize, and rice in all regions. Beans show stable trends with very slight positive yield anomalies (around 0.9% to 1.15%) in the Northern, Central, and Southern regions. Maize similarly demonstrates stable trends with positive yield anomalies ranging from 1.9% to 2.18%. Rice exhibits the highest percentage yield anomalies, notably 11.38% to 15.1%, indicating comparatively favorable conditions, especially in the Central Region. No regions are currently flagged as poor or watch for these crops, although rice shows higher variability suggesting some production uncertainty. Beans have the narrowest anomaly range and most consistent stability.
  • Kenya: Bean crop conditions in Kenya remain stable across all counties, with predicted yield anomalies near neutral, mostly between -0.43% and +0.95%, indicating steady performance without significant deviations. No alarming negative yield anomalies are recorded for beans, even in vulnerable counties. Maize conditions are generally stable as well, though there are localized negative yield anomalies, particularly in arid and semi-arid counties such as Garissa (-1.26%), Isiolo (-1.54%), Mandera (-2.18%), and Wajir (-2.51%), signaling some production stress. Rice shows mixed conditions with widespread negative yield anomalies, reaching up to -7.75% in West Pokot and notable declines in key producing counties like Baringo (-7.14%), Bungoma (-7.35%), and Kirinyaga (-6.17%), suggesting concerns for rice production outlook. Overall, maize and beans show stable trends, while rice conditions call for caution.

Production Narrative

The top-performing crop for the June 2026 season in Tanzania's bimodal regions is maize, with extensive production areas such as Geita (area 116,438 ha, mean production 222,397 tons), Shinyanga (149,364 ha, 158,326 tons), and Manyara (114,947 ha, 136,787 tons) leading outputs. Bean production is significant in regions like Simiyu with 19,676 ha producing an average of 5,116 tons, and Shinyanga with 21,174 ha yielding about 23,715 tons. Rice production is smaller comparatively but notable in Geita (20,768 ha, 69,988 tons) and Manyara (20,502 ha, 46,950 tons). Predicted production figures show some variability reflected in the broad minimum and maximum production ranges, indicating uncertainty primarily for rice with higher standard errors. The overall trend remains stable across all crops with no major shifts in predicted yields or production levels for the 2026 Masika season.

Figure: Agricultural Calendar – Malawi

Malawi is currently in the winter cropping season spanning from April to November, with planting initiated in April and harvest scheduled from September to November. This period represents the main harvest window across all regions, while preparations for the end of the season are underway.

Regional Crop Conditions Summary

Crop conditions across Malawi in 2026 remain stable for beans, maize, and rice in all regions. Beans show stable trends with very slight positive yield anomalies (around 0.9% to 1.15%) in the Northern, Central, and Southern regions. Maize similarly demonstrates stable trends with positive yield anomalies ranging from 1.9% to 2.18%. Rice exhibits the highest percentage yield anomalies, notably 11.38% to 15.1%, indicating comparatively favorable conditions, especially in the Central Region. No regions are currently flagged as poor or watch for these crops, although rice shows higher variability suggesting some production uncertainty. Beans have the narrowest anomaly range and most consistent stability.

Production Narrative

The top-performing crop by production area and predicted mean production is maize, with the Central Region leading at an estimated 2,218,089 tons over 901,662 hectares, followed by the Southern Region with 888,242 tons and the Northern Region at 739,737 tons. Rice shows the greatest variability despite smaller areas planted; the Central Region leads with mean production around 69,142 tons, Southern Region follows with 39,700 tons, and Northern Region produces 23,946 tons. All crops maintain stable trends through the forecast period, though rice's wide minimum to maximum production ranges indicate greater uncertainty.

Kenya

Figure: Agricultural Calendar – Kenya

Kenya is currently in the mid to late stage of the Long Rains season (February to August), with the planting phase completed and harvest expected to commence from July extending through August. This period marks the crop maturation phase, with farmers preparing for harvesting.

Regional Crop Conditions Summary

Bean crop conditions in Kenya remain stable across all counties, with predicted yield anomalies near neutral, mostly between -0.43% and +0.95%, indicating steady performance without significant deviations. No alarming negative yield anomalies are recorded for beans, even in vulnerable counties. Maize conditions are generally stable as well, though there are localized negative yield anomalies, particularly in arid and semi-arid counties such as Garissa (-1.26%), Isiolo (-1.54%), Mandera (-2.18%), and Wajir (-2.51%), signaling some production stress. Rice shows mixed conditions with widespread negative yield anomalies, reaching up to -7.75% in West Pokot and notable declines in key producing counties like Baringo (-7.14%), Bungoma (-7.35%), and Kirinyaga (-6.17%), suggesting concerns for rice production outlook. Overall, maize and beans show stable trends, while rice conditions call for caution.

Production Narrative

Beans are cultivated across varied regions with sizeable areas such as Kitui (132,905 ha), Narok (97,411 ha), and Uasin Gishu (91,656 ha), with mean predicted productions ranging from about 433 tons (e.g., Nyamira) to 146,196 tons (Kitui). Maize remains the top-producing crop with extensive cultivation areas and high predicted yields particularly in Uasin Gishu (181,360 ha, mean production 765,339 tons), Nakuru (211,050 ha, 574,056 tons), and Narok (192,746 ha, 275,627 tons). Rice production, by contrast, is limited in area but significant in key counties such as Baringo (629 ha, mean production 2,032 tons), Bungoma (1,438 ha, 2,689 tons), and Busia (395 ha, 1,059 tons). While maize dominates output, some counties face yield uncertainties with localized negative anomalies. Beans show stable production forecasts, and rice production exhibits downward pressure due to consistent negative yield anomalies.

Metrics Explanation

  • R² Score: Coefficient of determination (0–1); values above 0.7 indicate strong predictive power.
